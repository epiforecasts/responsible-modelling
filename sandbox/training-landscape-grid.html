Evaluation & responsibility in ID modelling training

How do existing training materials address evaluation and responsibility in infectious disease modelling? This table surveys major textbooks (5) and online courses (4) used to teach ID modelling. Sources identified from an AI web search of published tables of contents and syllabi (March 2026).

See Training landscape for a comprehensive overview of 25+ global training resources, showing how they sit on the spectrum from academic to applied, and where critical gaps exist (ethics, uncertainty communication, equity, outbreak response).

Methods

We searched for and reviewed the published chapter headings and course syllabi of five major ID modelling textbooks and four widely-used online courses. Classification was based on explicit course/chapter structure rather than text content: a topic was marked as present (●) if it appeared as a named chapter, unit, or module heading; partial (◑) if mentioned within broader topics (e.g., uncertainty mentioned within a methods chapter but not a standalone topic); absent (○) if not present in the published outline.

Textbooks reviewed: - Keeling & Rohani (2008) — Princeton - Vynnycky & White (2010) — OUP - Anderson & May (1991) — OUP - Bjornstad (2018/2022) — Springer - Martcheva (2015) — Springer

Online courses reviewed: - Imperial College London Coursera specialisation in ID modelling - Johns Hopkins Infectious Disease Modeling in Practice (2024) - University of Oxford Applied Methods in R - Handel IDEMA course materials (UGA, open access)

Key findings

The training landscape shows clear patterns:

  1. Universal foundation: Compartmental models (SIR/SEIR) and basic reproduction number (R₀) appear in virtually every resource. These are the entry point to all other topics.

  2. Academic-applied divide: Textbooks (Keeling & Rohani, Martcheva, Anderson & May) focus on mathematical rigour and theoretical analysis. Applied courses (Imperial CPD, Coursera for decision-makers) prioritise practical skills and policy relevance. Few resources bridge both equally.

  3. Methodological depth vs accessibility trade-off: Mathematical textbooks offer depth in analysis (stability, bifurcation theory, Lyapunov functions) but assume strong maths background. Software-focused courses (Graph Courses, EpiR Handbook, EpiModel) are more accessible but less rigorous.

  4. Emerging topics are siloed: Machine learning, genomic epidemiology, and ensemble forecasting appear only in specialist modules (mainly SISMID, ESPIDAM) rather than foundational courses.

  5. Critical gap in coverage:

    • Ethics & model transparency: 0 resources teach this formally
    • Uncertainty quantification: Limited systematic treatment
    • Communication to non-technical audiences: Only 1 dedicated course
    • Equity and access in modelling: Addressed in 1 resource
    • Real-time outbreak response: Barely formalised in any course

Resource summary by type

Textbooks (5)

Canonical references, deep mathematical treatment, used globally:

  • Keeling & Rohani (2008): Modeling Infectious Diseases in Humans and Animals — mathematical focus, chapters on dynamics, control, spatial models; no chapters on model evaluation or communication
  • Anderson & May (1991): Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control — comprehensive reference across microparasites and macroparasites; foundational but assumes strong biology background
  • Martcheva (2015): An Introduction to Mathematical Epidemiology — mathematically rigorous (ODE, PDE, age-structured models); includes fitting chapter; no coverage of policy or ethics
  • Vynnycky & White (2010): An Introduction to Infectious Disease Modelling — accessible to epidemiologists, practical orientation; limited coverage of stochastic and spatial methods
  • Bjornstad (2018/2022): Epidemics: Models and Data using R — unique in integrating R throughout; notable for time-series fitting chapters; bridges theory and practice

Intensive courses (7 major)

2–4 week residential programs, mix of lectures and hands-on sessions:

  • SISMID (Emory, USA) — gold standard for breadth; mathematical modules + emerging topics (ML, forecasting, simulation-based inference); attracts global audience
  • DAIDD (South Africa, rotating sites) — applied orientation, designed for researchers in low-resource settings; explicit focus on real-world constraints
  • ESPIDAM (Sweden, annual) — European summer program; mathematical + emerging topics (AI, phylodynamics); strong in inference and nowcasting
  • Imperial CPD (London, UK) — short courses for practitioners; applied epidemiology focus; explicitly designed for decision-makers and lab scientists
  • Harvard CCDD Workshop (Boston, USA) — annual mathematical modelling workshop; recent shift toward equity and diversity in modelling communities
  • ICTP-SAIFR (Brazil) — international program; mathematical rigour with developing-country focus; newer than SISMID/ESPIDAM
  • SPARK (Australia) — recently launched; details limited in this survey

Online courses (6 major)

Self-paced or short-duration, accessible globally:

  • Coursera: ID Transmission Models for Decision-Makers — explicitly targets non-technical policy stakeholders; rare resource bridging modelling and policy
  • Coursera: ID Modeling in Practice — balances methods and application; includes critical appraisal of published models
  • Coursera: HKU Epidemics — introduces basic concepts; accessible entry point
  • FUN-MOOC: Modeling of Infectious Diseases — French-language, open access; aims at accessibility
  • The GRAPH Courses: Introduction to ID Modelling — highly pedagogical, emphasis on software (R); designed for health professionals without maths background

Handbooks & reference materials (5)

Practical resources for implementation:

  • EpiR Handbook — collaborative living document; practical R for epidemiologists; covers fitting, forecasting, nowcasting
  • Handel IDEMA Book — open-access textbook; hybrid approach (maths + R + epidemiology); notably includes model uncertainty chapters
  • EpiModel Training — focused on network models in R; growing resource
  • EpiRecipe — Spanish-language, practical snippets for common problems
  • Epiverse-TRACE — open-source toolkit for outbreak analytics and modelling; tutorials and training focused on outbreak response workflows; recent development with emphasis on real-time applications

Community resources (3+)

Teaching materials, software tutorials, course repositories:

  • GitHub repositories (Höhle, Firth, NFIDD) — course materials, code, tutorials; transparency and reproducibility focus
  • YouTube playlists — lectures and walkthroughs
  • Interactive visualizations — SPARK, others

Orientation questions for training design

If you’re developing new training in ID modelling, consider where it sits:

  1. Audience: Research scientists? Policy advisors? Lab technicians? Health planners? Modelling practitioners in low-resource settings?
  2. Mathematical depth: How much mathematical rigour is needed vs. intuition and hands-on practice?
  3. Disciplinary grounding: Is the focus epidemiological, mathematical, statistical, computational, or public health?
  4. Applied orientation: Is the goal to understand principles, implement methods, or inform decisions?
  5. Which gaps does it fill? The landscape above shows what’s well-covered (basic compartmental models) and what’s thin (ethics, communication, equity).
